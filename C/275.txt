       <h1 class="heading-1__sizing">Efficiency</h1> 
       <p>When you use Google products, the servers in our data centers do the work for you — around the clock and around the world. Our servers support many products at a time. That's “the cloud.” By keeping our servers busy, we can do more with less — more searches, more Gmail, and more YouTube videos with fewer servers and less energy. We’ve worked hard to minimize the environmental impact of these services so that when you use our products, you’re also being good to the environment.</p> 
      </div> 
     </div> 
    </div>

       <h2 class="heading-2__sizing">Helping businesses save energy in the cloud</h2> 
       <p>The cloud supports many products at a time, so it can more efficiently distribute resources among many users. That means we can do more with less energy — and businesses can too. In 2013, Lawrence Berkeley National Laboratory published <a href="https://crd.lbl.gov/assets/pubs_presos/ACS/cloud_efficiency_study.pdf" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">research</a> indicating that moving all office workers in the United States to the cloud could reduce the energy used by information technology by up to 87%.</p> 
       <p>Related specifically to Google products, a <a href="https://static.googleusercontent.com/media/www.google.com/en/us/green/pdf/google-apps.pdf" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">case study of the U.S. General Services Administration (GSA)</a> showed that by switching to Google Apps, they were able to reduce office computing costs, energy use, and carbon emissions by 65-90%. Additionally, <a href="https://static.googleusercontent.com/media/www.google.com/en/us/green/pdfs/google-green-computing.pdf" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">our study</a> has shown that businesses that use Gmail have decreased the environmental impact of their email service by up to 98% compared to those that run email on local servers.</p> 
       <p>Because of our energy efficiency efforts, our cloud is better for the environment. This means businesses that use our cloud-based products are greener too.</p>

   <section class="measure-and-improve"> 
    <div class="h-c-page"> 
     <h2 class="adjust-1__size">Measuring and improving our energy use</h2> 
     <p>We're focused on reducing our energy use while serving the explosive growth of the internet. Most data centers use almost as much non-computing or "overhead" energy (like cooling and power conversion) as they do to power their servers. At Google we've reduced this overhead to only 11%. That way, most of the energy we use powers the machines directly serving Google searches and products. We take detailed measurements to continually push toward doing more with less — serving more users while wasting less energy.</p> 
     <div class="container">

        <div class="center-aligned-content information big-paragraph h-c-grid__col h-c-grid__col--12 h-c-grid__col-l--6 side-spacing"> 
         <h2 class="heading-2__sizing">We take the most comprehensive approach to measuring Power Usage Effectiveness (PUE)</h2> 
         <p>Our calculations include the performance of our entire fleet of data centers around the world — not just our newest and best facilities. We also continuously measure throughout the year — not just during cooler seasons.</p> 
         <p>Additionally, we include all sources of overhead in our efficiency metric. We could report much lower numbers if we took the loosest interpretation of the <a href="https://www.thegreengrid.org/en/resources/library-and-tools/20-PUE:-A-Comprehensive-Examination-of-the-Metric" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">Green Grid's PUE measurement standards</a>. In fact, our best site could boast a PUE of less than 1.06 if we used an interpretation commonly used in the industry. However, we're sticking to a higher standard because we believe it's better to measure and optimize everything on our site, not just part of it. Therefore, we report a comprehensive trailing twelve-month (TTM) PUE of 1.10 across all our large-scale data centers (once they reach stable operations), in all seasons, including all sources of overhead.</p>

       </div> 
      </div> 
     </div>

    <div class="h-c-page faq-zippy"> 
     <div class="glue-zippy glue-a-zippy--v-smooth-expand" id="basic-zippy-effects" ng-cloak> 
      <div class="faq-question glue-zippy-btn"> 
       <h3 class="adjust-2__size">What's the average PUE of other data centers?</h3> 
       <svg aria-hidden="true" role="presentation" class="h-c-icon--18px h-c-expansion-panels__button-arrow"> <use xlink:href="#mi-arrow-item"></use> 
       </svg> 
      </div> 
      <div class="glue-zippy-content" data-glue-zippy-content-set-aria-hidden="true"> 
       <p>According to the <a href="https://uptimeinstitute.com/resources/asset/2019-data-center-industry-survey" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">Uptime Institute's 2019 Data Center Survey</a>, the global average of respondents' largest data centers is around 1.67.</p> 
      </div> 
     </div> 
     <div class="glue-zippy glue-a-zippy--v-smooth-expand" id="basic-zippy-effects" ng-cloak> 
      <div class="faq-question glue-zippy-btn"> 
       <h3 class="adjust-2__size">Why does our data vary?</h3> 
       <svg aria-hidden="true" role="presentation" class="h-c-icon--18px h-c-expansion-panels__button-arrow"> <use xlink:href="#mi-arrow-item"></use> 
       </svg> 
      </div> 
      <div class="glue-zippy-content" data-glue-zippy-content-set-aria-hidden="true"> 
       <p>Our facilities have different power and cooling infrastructures, and are located in different climates. Seasonal weather patterns also impact PUE values, which is why they tend to be lower during cooler quarters. We've managed to maintain a low PUE average across our entire fleet of data center sites around the world — even during hot, humid summers.</p> 
      </div> 
     </div> 
     <div class="glue-zippy glue-a-zippy--v-smooth-expand" id="basic-zippy-effects" ng-cloak> 
      <div class="faq-question glue-zippy-btn"> 
       <h3 class="adjust-2__size">How do we get our PUE data?</h3> 
       <svg aria-hidden="true" role="presentation" class="h-c-icon--18px h-c-expansion-panels__button-arrow"> <use xlink:href="#mi-arrow-item"></use> 
       </svg> 
      </div> 
      <div class="glue-zippy-content" data-glue-zippy-content-set-aria-hidden="true"> 
       <p>We use multiple on-line power meters in our data centers to measure power consumption over time. We track the energy used by our cooling infrastructure and IT equipment on separate meters, giving us very accurate PUE calculations. We account for all of our power-consuming elements in our PUE by using dozens or even hundreds of power meters in our facilities.</p> 
      </div> 
     </div> 
     <div class="glue-zippy glue-a-zippy--v-smooth-expand" id="basic-zippy-effects" ng-cloak> 
      <div class="faq-question glue-zippy-btn"> 
       <h3 class="adjust-2__size">What do we include in our calculations?</h3> 
       <svg aria-hidden="true" role="presentation" class="h-c-icon--18px h-c-expansion-panels__button-arrow"> <use xlink:href="#mi-arrow-item"></use> 
       </svg> 
      </div> 
      <div class="glue-zippy-content" data-glue-zippy-content-set-aria-hidden="true"> 
       <p>When measuring our IT equipment power, we include only the servers, storage, and networking equipment. We consider everything else overhead power. For example, we include electrical losses from a server's power cord as overhead, not as IT power. Similarly, we measure total utility power at the utility side of the substation, and therefore include substation transformer losses in our PUE.</p>

        <h4 class="h-c-tile__headline heading-4__sizing">Power Usage Effectiveness (PUE)</h4> 
        <p class="h-c-tile__description">The data center industry uses the measurement PUE, or power usage effectiveness, to measure efficiency. A PUE of 2.0 means that for every watt of IT power, an additional watt is consumed to cool and distribute power to the IT equipment. A PUE closer to 1.0 means nearly all of the energy is used for computing.</p>
