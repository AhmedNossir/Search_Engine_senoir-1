    <h3 id="what-does-users-over-license-mean">What does "users over license" mean?</h3> 
    <p>If you've added more users to your GitLab EE instance during the past period than you were licensed for, the additional users will be payable at the time of renewal.</p> 
    <p>Without adding these users during the renewal process, your license key will not work.</p> 
    <p>You can find the number of <strong>users over license</strong> by going to the <code>/admin</code> section of your GitLab instance (e.g. <code>https://example.gitlab.com/admin</code>). This can also be found by clicking the <strong>admin wrench</strong> in the navbar of your instance when logged in as an admin.</p> 
    <p>In the top right section of the admin dashboard, you should see the number to enter when asked this during the renewal process.</p>

    <h3 id="who-gets-counted-in-the-subscription">Who gets counted in the subscription?</h3> 
    <h4 class="no_toc" id="gitlabcom-1">GitLab.com</h4> 
    <p>Each unique user within a namespace is counted in a subscription. This includes users added at the group level, sub-group level and project level. Every occupied seat, whether by person, job or bot is counted in the subscription. The only exception are <a href="https://docs.gitlab.com/ee/user/permissions.html#free-guest-users-ultimate" target="_blank">members with <code>Guest</code> permissions with an Ultimate subscription</a>.</p> 
    <p>Since GitLab.com counts concurrent seats and not named users, you can remove members and add new members as you'd like as long as the total users at any given time is within your license count.</p> 
    <h4 class="no_toc" id="self-managed-gitlab-1">Self-managed GitLab</h4> 
    <p>Every occupied seat, whether by person, administrator, job or bot is counted in the subscription.</p> 
    <p>The following are the only exceptions which are not counted towards the subscription:</p> 
    <ol> 
     <li>Blocked users who are blocked prior to the renewal of a subscription will <strong>not</strong> be counted as Active Users for the renewal subscription but <strong>may</strong> count as true-up users for the term in which they were originally added.</li> 
     <li><a href="https://docs.gitlab.com/ee/user/permissions.html#free-guest-users-ultimate" target="_blank">Members with <code>Guest</code> permissions on an Ultimate subscription</a> do not count towards the subscription.</li> 
     <li><a href="https://docs.gitlab.com/ee/user/profile/account/delete_account.html#associated-records" target="_blank">Ghost User</a>, <a href="https://docs.gitlab.com/ee/user/project/service_desk.html#support-bot-user" target="_blank">Support Bot</a>, <code>Migration Bot</code>, and <code>Alert Bot</code> do not count towards the subscription.</li> 
    </ol>

    <h3 id="how-are-maximum-users-calculated">How are Maximum Users Calculated?</h3> 
    <p>In <a href="https://gitlab.com/gitlab-org/gitlab/-/issues/7008">GitLab 13.7 and later</a>, the <code>Maximum Users</code> value found in self-managed instances of GitLab reflects the maximum daily active user count recorded in the instance during the current license period.</p> 
    <p>Prior to GitLab 13.7, the <code>Maximum Users</code> value found in self-managed instances of GitLab reflects the maximum daily active user count recorded in the instance for the period of 1 year prior to the expiration date of the license. Since most GitLab subscriptions are annual, this means the Maximum User count in these cases is simply the highest number of active users at any one time during that subscription. However, for non-standard contract lengths (8 months, 14 months, 24 months), the Maximum Users count is not reflective of the entire subscription term, but rather for the 12 months prior to the expiration date.</p>

    <p>Nope! The <code>root</code> user is just the first admin account, created by default in self-managed versions of GitLab. Just like any other user, this account <em>does</em> occupy a license seat. So, please consider following good security practice and have one or more "real" people play the role of administrator. You're allowed (and encouraged) to rename the user, or even delete or disable it as long as you've promoted other users to administrator.</p>

    <h3 id="whats-the-difference-between-a-group-plan-and-a-personal-plan-on-gitlabcom">What's the difference between a Group plan and a Personal plan on GitLab.com?</h3> 
    <p>A subscription for GitLab.com can be applied to one of two types of namespaces. Where you assign your subscription determines where those features are accessible.</p> 
    <h4 class="no_toc" id="gitlabcom-saas-plan-on-personal-namespace">GitLab.com SaaS Plan on Personal Namespace</h4> 
    <p>If a subscription is applied to a personal account then that account will have access to the features of the subscription for all of the projects they create under their personal account. If they want, other users can be invited to those projects and they'll be able to enjoy the features of that subscription <em>only while working on those projects</em>. If that user then creates a group, it will by default be on the <strong>Free plan</strong>.</p> 
    <p>A user that never collaborates with others on GitLab may opt to purchase a subscription for their personal user account since they have no need for a group and will always only work in projects under their personal account.</p> 
    <p>You can find the plan details for a personal namespace by navigating the <strong>User Settings&gt;Account&gt;Billing</strong>.</p> 
    <h4 class="no_toc" id="gitlabcom-saas-plan-on-a-group">GitLab.com SaaS Plan on a Group</h4> 
    <p>A user can choose to purchase a subscription and apply it to a group they've created. This way any project they create in that group or in a subgroup of that group gets access to the features of the subscription they purchased for it. This extends to any user that gets invited as a member of that group.</p> 
    <p>A user that's part of an organization with multiple GitLab collaborators will ideally choose to create a group for that organization, purchase and apply a subscription to that group, and then invite their colleagues to that group so that all can enjoy those paid features while working in that group.</p> 
    <p>Note that all members within a group subscription are counted as billable seats at the same subscription plan rate.</p> 
    <p>You can find the plan details for a group namespace by navigating the <strong>Group Settings&gt;Account&gt;Billing</strong>.</p>

    <h3 id="how-can-i-manage-multiple-teams-in-one-gitlabcom-saas-groupplan-but-prevent-the-teams-from-accessing-each-others-projects">How can I manage multiple teams in one GitLab.com SaaS group/plan but prevent the teams from accessing each other's projects?</h3> 
    <p>You can create a parent group and only allow access to this group to anyone who should have unlimited child subgroup/project access. Then, create subgroups for each team with nested projects and add people at the subgroup level. This will essentially lock them out of any other team's subgroups for which they do not have access.</p> 
    <p>In this situation, regarding billable users - you will only be responsible for the unique users within the hierarchy of the parent group. If a user is added to multiple subgroups or projects, they will only be counted as 1 billable user.</p>
