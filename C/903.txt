      <p><b>Openclipart</b> is an online <a href="/wiki/Digital_library" title="Digital library">media repository</a> of <a href="/wiki/Free-content" class="mw-redirect" title="Free-content">free-content</a> <a href="/wiki/Vector_graphics" title="Vector graphics">vector</a> <a href="/wiki/Clip_art" title="Clip art">clip art</a>. The project hosts over 150,000 free graphics and has billed itself as "the largest community of artists making the best free original clipart for you to use for absolutely any reason." All of Openclipart's content is <a href="/wiki/Public_domain" title="Public domain">public domain</a> due to expired <a href="/wiki/Copyright" title="Copyright">copyright</a>, failing to reach the <a href="/wiki/Threshold_of_originality" title="Threshold of originality">threshold of originality</a>, or being <a href="/wiki/Public_domain_equivalent_license" class="mw-redirect" title="Public domain equivalent license">deeded into the public domain</a> to the greatest legal extent possible by its owners.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability"><span title="The material near this tag failed verification of its source citation(s). (September 2020)">failed verification</span></a></i>]</sup> </p>
      <p>The website was brought down for several months by a reported <a href="/wiki/Denial-of-service_attack" title="Denial-of-service attack">DDoS attack</a> in April 2019;<sup id="cite_ref-Finding_an_OpenClipart_fallback_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Finding_an_OpenClipart_fallback-2">[2]</a></sup> access to the library via other means was still possible, including partial copies of the library that were hosted on several <a href="/wiki/Mirror_site" title="Mirror site">mirror sites</a>. The site's search feature was restored in May 2020.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> </p>

      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Openclipart&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>The Openclipart library (OCAL) was established in early 2004 by Jon Phillips and Bryce Harrington, who had worked together to develop the <a href="/wiki/Open-source_software" title="Open-source software">open-source</a> vector graphics suites <a href="/wiki/Sodipodi" title="Sodipodi">Sodipodi</a> and its successor, <a href="/wiki/Inkscape" title="Inkscape">Inkscape</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The OCAL project initially grew out of a project started by Christian Schaller (Uraeus), who, on October 26, 2003, issued a challenge on the Gnome Desktop website for Sodipodi users to create a collection of <a href="/wiki/Flags" class="mw-redirect" title="Flags">flags</a> in <a href="/wiki/SVG_format" class="mw-redirect" title="SVG format">SVG format</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> The flag project progressed very well, resulting in a collection of over 90 flags made publicly available in SVG format,<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> and broadened the project's goals to include generic clipart. The project became known as Openclipart by April 2004, with the stated aim of making all its contributed images freely available in the <a href="/wiki/Public_domain" title="Public domain">public domain</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>In the early stages of the Openclipart project, the website lacked thumbnails and was difficult to browse. Downloadable Openclipart packages were released to help propagate the images in the library, and were available directly from the Openclipart website: as an add-on for various <a href="/wiki/Linux_distribution" title="Linux distribution">Linux distributions</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Fedora_(operating_system)" title="Fedora (operating system)">Fedora</a>, and as an NSIS installer for <a href="/wiki/Windows" class="mw-redirect" title="Windows">Windows</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-12">[12]</a></sup> Each package included most of the clipart to date, and they were manually sorted into categories.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-12">[12]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> The Openclipart package version 0.20 was released in 2010.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> The Openclipart packages received a few more incremental updates during 2010, mostly for seasonal clipart.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>An overhauled Openclipart 2.0 website went live as a beta in February 2010 with a full release in March 2010.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> The site introduced a change from the old <a href="/wiki/CcHost" title="CcHost">ccHost</a> software to the new <a href="/wiki/Affero_General_Public_License" title="Affero General Public License">AGPL</a>-based <a href="/wiki/Aiki_Framework" title="Aiki Framework">Aiki Framework</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> a content management system made for Openclipart 2.0. The new site allowed anyone to easily browse and add to the Openclipart collection. Jon Phillips, Andy Fitzsimon, <a href="/wiki/Bassel_Safadi" class="mw-redirect" title="Bassel Safadi">Bassel Safadi</a>, Michi, Ronaldo Barbachano, and Brad Phillips added image <a href="/wiki/Thumbnail" title="Thumbnail">thumbnails</a> and improved search functions made the Openclipart library more user-friendly, which contributed to higher site traffic. The site was receiving over 5,000 unique visitors and 50,000 page views daily.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>The 3.0 website release incorporated allowed members to "favorite" clip art, and provided an image-editing feature that made image remixing significantly easier. </p>
      <p>On April 15, 2013, Openclipart launched a new logo and updated their website design with a "scissors" logo.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Writing_better_articles#Stay_on_topic" title="Wikipedia:Writing better articles"><span title="The material near this tag may contain information that is not relevant to the article's main topic. (September 2020)">relevant?</span></a></i>]</sup><sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>On March 12, 2014, Openclipart announced that Inkpad, an open-source drawing app for <a href="/wiki/IPad" title="IPad">iPads</a>, had released library integration to make the entire collection available to its users.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> </p> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Lockdown_and_attempts_at_mirroring_the_library">Lockdown and attempts at mirroring the library</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Openclipart&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Lockdown and attempts at mirroring the library">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>On April 19, 2019, the site was taken offline by what was initially reported as a <a href="/wiki/Distributed_denial-of-service_attack" class="mw-redirect" title="Distributed denial-of-service attack">distributed denial-of-service attack</a> (DDoS attack),<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Finding_an_OpenClipart_fallback_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Finding_an_OpenClipart_fallback-2">[2]</a></sup> though Jon Philips, one of the site's founders, appeared to be a victim of <a href="/wiki/Identity_theft" title="Identity theft">identity theft</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> All pages on OpenClipart were redirected to a page asking users to donate money "to support protection". The message was updated on December 25, 2019, with a statement that the site was being "gifted to the community" and that new files could once again be uploaded to the library. </p>
      <p>By <a href="/wiki/Web_scraping" title="Web scraping">scraping</a>, most of the library was recovered and hosted on FreeSVG.org.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> A second mirror was established at FreeSVGClipArt.com, which took advantage of the clips' lack of licensing restrictions.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>In early May 2020, the website's Twitter account announced that the search feature had been re-enabled. </p>

       <li>Linux distributions</li>
      </ul> 
      <p>Some <a href="/wiki/Linux_distribution" title="Linux distribution">Linux distributions</a>, including <a href="/wiki/Mandriva" title="Mandriva">Mandriva</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ubuntu" title="Ubuntu">Ubuntu</a>, include many of the Openclipart collection releases packaged as SVG, PNG or <a href="/wiki/OpenDocument" title="OpenDocument">OpenDocument</a>-format files.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> These distributions are based on the 2005 pre-ccHost release, since regular releases stopped after the switch to ccHost software. Openclipart 0.19, the first version released after the switch, was released in March 2009. With the release of version 2.0 and updated packages, distributions have bugs filed in their respective bug trackers to begin packaging Openclipart once more. </p>
      <p>Openclipart was included on the cover discs in <i><a href="/wiki/Linux_Format" title="Linux Format">Linux Format</a></i> issues 123 and 132 as a package of browseable SVG files from the Openclipart collection.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> </p>
